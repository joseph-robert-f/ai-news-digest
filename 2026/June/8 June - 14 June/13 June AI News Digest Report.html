AnthropicCommerce Department orders Anthropic to suspend Fable 5 and Mythos 5 — three days after launch

12–13 June 2026 · Primary sources: Anthropic newsroom / U.S. Department of Commerce directive (via Axios) / CNBC / NBC News

At 5:21 PM ET on Friday 12 June, Anthropic received a letter from the U.S. Department of Commerce signed by Secretary Howard Lutnick invoking export-control authority and ordering an immediate suspension of access to Claude Fable 5 and Claude Mythos 5 for any foreign national, inside or outside the United States. Anthropic announced compliance within hours, taking access offline for all users globally on the basis that real-time enforcement by nationality is technically infeasible and would risk over- and under-blocking. The two models had been publicly released only three days earlier on 9 June — Fable 5 as the consumer-facing, safety-tuned member of the Mythos family, with Mythos 5 retained for closed-partner cybersecurity work (an evolution of the Project Glasswing program covered in the 27 May digest). The Commerce Department cites a specific jailbreak technique that an unnamed third party demonstrated to the government; according to Anthropic, the demonstration showed only the model identifying a small number of previously known, minor vulnerabilities in a codebase it was asked to review. Anthropic's public statement calls the action disproportionate, warns that applying the same standard industry-wide would halt all frontier-model deployment, and says it is working to restore access. The order represents the first time Washington has forced a commercial AI product offline — a structurally new fact for the frontier-lab industry, and one that will shape every downstream conversation about model-deployment governance, dual-use export controls under EAR/ECCN, and the new National Security Memorandum on AI.

OpenAIAcquires Ona (née Gitpod) to run Codex agents inside the customer's own cloud

11 June 2026 · Primary sources: OpenAI newsroom (openai.com/index/openai-to-acquire-ona/) / Bloomberg / CNBC

OpenAI announced on Thursday 11 June that it has agreed to acquire Ona — the Kiel-based developer-tools company that operated as Gitpod until its late-2025 rebrand — to absorb its secure cloud-execution platform into the Codex team. Ona's core product runs coding agents in long-lived, sandboxed environments that sit inside the customer's own cloud account (or on the customer's own infrastructure) with the IAM, network, and data-residency posture the customer already trusts. That solves the practical bottleneck behind the current generation of Codex deployments: large enterprises will not let an OpenAI-hosted agent reach production source control, build systems, or staging databases. Post-deal, Codex users will be able to delegate multi-hour or multi-day coding tasks to an agent that persists in the customer's environment without being tied to an active ChatGPT session or single device. OpenAI says more than 5 million people now use Codex weekly. Purchase price was not disclosed; closing is subject to regulatory approval. The deal slots cleanly alongside the OpenAI–Oracle Universal Credits (UCM) distribution arrangement also announced this week, under which Oracle Cloud Infrastructure customers can deploy Codex against their existing Oracle commit.

Open-Source AI & Research

Google DeepMindGemma 4 12B: encoder-free multimodal (text/image/audio/video) model on a 16 GB laptop, Apache 2.0

Released 3 June, gaining adoption momentum this week · Primary sources: huggingface.co/google/gemma-4-12B / MarkTechPost / Google blog

Gemma 4 12B — released earlier this month and widely adopted by community fine-tuners over the past 48 hours — is a 12-billion-parameter, encoder-free, natively multimodal decoder-only transformer that handles text, images, audio and video without separate vision or audio encoders, and runs on any system with 16 GB of RAM or VRAM. Google ships three artifacts on Hugging Face and Kaggle: the base gemma-4-12B, the chat-tuned gemma-4-12B-it, and a gemma-4-12B-it-assistant draft model for speculative decoding (MTP) — the first time a major open-weights release has shipped a paired draft model in the same drop. License is Apache 2.0, with unrestricted commercial deployment rights. The encoder-free design is the more interesting research thread here: stripping out the vision/audio encoders meaningfully shrinks the parameter budget and removes a long-standing source of modality-mismatch failures in multimodal benchmarks. The model materially raises the floor for what a single-developer laptop deployment can run, and lands squarely in the laptop-class niche that Mistral and Apple Intelligence had been competing for.

Google ResearchTurboQuant @ ICLR 2026: PolarQuant + Quantized Johnson–Lindenstrauss compresses the KV cache

ICLR 2026 paper, reporting wave 11–13 June · Primary source: Google Research blog / ICLR 2026 proceedings

Google's research team detailed TurboQuant at ICLR 2026 this week, a two-step technique that materially cuts the memory overhead of the KV cache — the single largest bottleneck in running long-context, batched LLM inference. Step one is PolarQuant, a vector-rotation step that re-aligns the KV-cache distribution before quantisation; step two is a quantised Johnson–Lindenstrauss compression that exploits the now-aligned geometry to shrink the cache without measurable quality loss. The practical implication is straightforward: for the same hardware, you can serve longer context windows or larger batches at the same latency; or alternatively, you can serve the existing workload at a meaningfully smaller GPU memory footprint — relevant for both hyperscalers fighting GB200/MI355X capacity constraints and developers running Gemma 4 12B locally. The technique is model-agnostic and is being upstreamed into Google's serving stack first; an open paper and reference implementation are expected to follow.

MicrosoftWSL Kernel 6.18.35.1 fixes the 4 GB virtio-fs Docker build truncation bug

12 June 2026 · Primary source: WSL release notes / Windows News

Microsoft shipped WSL Kernel 6.18.35.1 on 12 June, finally fixing a long-standing bug that had made docker build on WSL2 unreliable for any image whose ADD or COPY instruction touched a file larger than 4 GB over the virtio-fs share. Under the old kernel, the build would either truncate the source file or fail midway through layer creation, with no consistent error. With the fix, docker build --memory=8g can copy a 6.2 GB SQL Server backup file into a layer cleanly. The bug had been particularly painful for three concrete workloads: Unreal Engine asset packs, large SQLite databases used for offline analytics, and pandas DataFrames serialised with to_parquet() — all routinely >4 GB and all routinely baked into images on developer laptops. The release also folds in Greg Kroah-Hartman's parallel 6.18.35 stable kernel push (alongside 6.12.93 and 7.0.12), keeping Microsoft's WSL kernel within striking distance of upstream LTS.
