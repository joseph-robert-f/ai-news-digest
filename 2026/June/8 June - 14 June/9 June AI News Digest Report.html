Stanford HAIPsychAdapter: steering LLM outputs to mimic specific personality, age, and mental-health profiles

8 June 2026 · Primary source: Stanford HAI news, 8 Jun 2026

Stanford's Human-Centered AI institute published a PsychAdapter writeup yesterday — a method that lets researchers dial in specific personality traits (the Big Five), age cohorts, and mental-health characteristics, and then have an LLM generate text that reads as if produced by an individual matching that profile. Stated use cases are training simulations for clinicians, controlled-cohort behavioral studies, and personalized content generation for therapy adjuncts; the method is also obviously dual-use for adversarial work on persuasion and social-engineering benchmarks. HAI published alongside a paired piece, “Exploring the dangers of AI in mental health care,” which reports a two-experiment study of five popular therapy chatbots (including 7cups' Pi and Noni, and Character.ai's Therapist) measuring stigma toward mental-health conditions and response appropriateness to common symptoms. Lead researcher Haber's framing: “this isn't simply ‘LLMs for therapy is bad,’ it's asking us to think critically about the role of LLMs in therapy.” The combined release is a notable signal that the academic community is moving from generic LLM-safety framings toward instrument-grade methods for evaluating psychiatric-specific failure modes — relevant to any product team shipping wellness, coaching, or companion features in the next 12 months.

arXivRecent reasoning-LLM papers worth a read: latent reward steering, neuro-symbolic math verification, and quantized reasoning

June 2026 listings · Primary source: arXiv cs.AI / cs.LG current

Three June arXiv preprints are circulating on developer-research timelines this week. (1) Latent Reward Steering proposes an inference-time framework that implicitly promotes cognitive behaviors (backtracking, self-correction, sub-goal decomposition) in reasoning models without RLHF retraining; the claim is that small steering vectors applied to hidden states recover much of the gain RL-tuned reasoning models get from post-training. (2) AXIOM outlines a trust-first neuro-symbolic execution architecture for verifiable mathematical reasoning — a direct response to the “reasoning trace looks right but the answer is wrong” failure mode that dogged GPT-o-series and Claude-thinking outputs in late 2025. (3) Quantized Reasoning Models Think They Need to Think Longer, but They Do Not (Lotfi, Kirichenko, Li, Liu) reports that quantization to int4/int8 systematically inflates reasoning-token budgets at no accuracy benefit — a meaningful finding for anyone running cost-optimized self-host stacks of DeepSeek-R-class models. Read in conjunction with this week's BAGEN benchmark (“Are LLM Agents Budget-Aware?”), the through-line is a research-community shift toward economics as a first-class evaluation axis — how much thinking is enough, and who pays for it.
