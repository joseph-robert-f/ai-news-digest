Top Story — Mythos-Class Goes Public

AnthropicClaude Fable 5 ships to the public as Anthropic's most powerful generally-available model; Mythos 5 stays inside Project Glasswing

9 June 2026 · Primary source: Anthropic Newsroom (anthropic.com/news/claude-fable-5-mythos-5)

Anthropic released Claude Fable 5 on Tuesday 9 June, the first Mythos-class model the company has put into general availability. Anthropic's own framing: "Today we're launching Claude Fable 5: a Mythos-class model that we've made safe for general use." Capability claims include state-of-the-art results across software engineering, knowledge work, vision, and scientific research, with always-on adaptive thinking, a 1 M-token context window, up to 128 K output tokens, and a fresh layer of safety classifiers that re-route a small slice of high-risk queries to Claude Opus 4.8 (Anthropic says the classifiers fire on < 5% of sessions). List pricing is $10 / 1 M input tokens and $50 / 1 M output tokens, roughly 2× Opus 4.8, positioning Fable 5 for institutional and reasoning-heavy workloads. Simultaneously, Anthropic put the un-restricted sibling — Claude Mythos 5 — into limited release via Project Glasswing, the ~50-org defensive-cybersecurity consortium covered in last month's digest cycle. Notably, this release lands only days after Anthropic publicly cautioned that frontier capability gains are outpacing alignment work; the company is explicitly framing the Fable/Mythos split as the structural answer to that gap — release a hardened sibling broadly, keep the unrestricted model under partner-bound access controls. The move makes Anthropic the first lab to ship a generally-available model above the Opus / GPT-5.5 / Gemini 3.5 Pro tier.

AppleWWDC 2026: Siri rebuilt on a custom 1.2 T-parameter Gemini; Claude and ChatGPT become first-class “Extensions”

8 June 2026 (Monday keynote) · Primary source: Apple Newsroom / Apple Developer / Craig Federighi remarks

Apple's WWDC 2026 keynote in Cupertino on Monday 8 June ended a two-year speculation cycle. The rebuilt Siri — shipping in iOS 27, iPadOS 27, and macOS 27 — runs on a custom 1.2 trillion-parameter Google Gemini model trained for Apple and served through Apple's Private Cloud Compute (PCC). Multiple outlets report Apple is paying ~$1 B / year for the arrangement. The 2026 wrinkle is that PCC now runs on NVIDIA Blackwell GPUs hosted inside Google Cloud, with Apple's same end-to-end data-handling guarantees layered on top — Apple's framing is that Google can see traffic shape but not content. The second, arguably bigger, announcement is Siri Extensions: a new framework that lets users route queries to Claude, ChatGPT, or Gemini as first-class targets from inside Siri itself, with a redesigned standalone Siri app that mirrors ChatGPT / Claude / Gemini's chat UI and adds an "Ask Siri" text bar. For Apple this is a structural concession — the OS no longer arbitrates a single LLM — and for Anthropic it is the biggest distribution win in the company's history: hundreds of millions of iPhone users will be one toggle away from Claude. Apple separately unveiled a refreshed Apple Foundation Models family for on-device tasks, but the cloud reasoning surface is now Gemini-first by default.

MiniMaxMiniMax M3: the first open-weight model to combine frontier coding, 1 M context, and native multimodality

1 June 2026 · Primary source: MiniMax Research

Shanghai-based MiniMax released MiniMax M3 on 1 June via API and the MiniMax Agent surface, framed as the first open-weight model to combine frontier-tier coding, a 1 M-token context window, and native multimodal computer-use. The architectural novelty is MSA (MiniMax Sparse Attention), an attention variant designed to push effective context to 1 M tokens without quadratic cost. Self-reported numbers on SWE-Bench Pro: 59.0%, edging GPT-5.5 (58.6%), beating Gemini 3.1 Pro (54.2%), and trailing Claude Opus 4.7 (64.3%) — with the obvious caveat that Opus 4.8's 69.2% on the same benchmark (released after M3's eval window) remains the public leader. Two caveats are worth flagging: (1) the model is "open-weight" in intent — the actual weights and technical report were still pending public release as of 9 June; and (2) every benchmark was produced on MiniMax's own infrastructure with scaffolding it chose (largely Claude Code), so the headline numbers await independent reproduction. Even with those caveats, M3 collapses one of the remaining gaps between proprietary and open frontiers: an open-weight stack that plausibly matches GPT-5.5 on competitive coding, at 5–10% of the per-token cost, is a meaningful pressure point on the closed labs' inference pricing.

Policy & Regulatory Signals

US CongressBipartisan “Great American AI Act” discussion draft would preempt state AI law for three years

4 June 2026 · Primary source: Office of Rep. Jay Obernolte (R-CA) & Rep. Lori Trahan (D-MA) joint release

Reps. Jay Obernolte (R-CA) and Lori Trahan (D-MA) released a bipartisan discussion draft of the Great American AI Act on Wednesday 4 June, the most concrete federal-framework proposal yet from the House. Four load-bearing provisions: (1) a three-year preemption of state AI laws — the same mechanism a federal court already used in April to pause Colorado's now-revised act — explicitly designed to remove state-by-state fragmentation; (2) statutory codification of NIST's Center for AI Standards and Innovation at $100 M / year for FY 2027–2029; (3) frontier-developer duties for any lab with >$500 M in prior-year revenue (mandatory public frontier-AI safety frameworks; $1 M per day in liability for safety-requirement violations); and (4) mandatory engagement of an NIST-licensed Independent Verification Organization (IVO) running semi-annual compliance verifications. For frontier labs, this is the first federal proposal that explicitly imports the EU-style "systemic risk" obligation into US law via a US-only licensing layer. For state attorneys general, it directly conflicts with bills already moving in California and New York. The draft is open for stakeholder feedback before formal introduction, but its bipartisan posture — on a topic that has been almost uniformly partisan since 2024 — makes it the most realistic federal AI vehicle of the cycle.
