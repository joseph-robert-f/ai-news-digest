OpenAIGPT‑5.5‑Cyber tops CyberGym at 85.6% — ahead of the still-suspended Mythos 5

22 June 2026 · Primary sources: OpenAI Daybreak release; Axios; CyberPress; CybersecurityNews

OpenAI on Monday 22 June released GPT‑5.5‑Cyber, the cybersecurity-specialised member of the Daybreak family, available only to verified security professionals. The headline benchmark is CyberGym — a 1,507-vulnerability harness across 188 open-source projects that measures whether an agent can reproduce known vulnerabilities in controlled environments. GPT‑5.5‑Cyber scored 85.6%, up from base GPT‑5.5 at 81.8%, ahead of Anthropic's Mythos 5 at 83.8% (still suspended for foreign nationals under the 12 June Commerce directive — see the 15 June digest) and well ahead of Claude Opus 4 at 73.1%. Side metrics: 39.5% on ExploitGym (exploit synthesis from a known vuln; GPT‑5.5 was 25.95%) and 69.8% on SEC‑bench Pro (long-horizon discovery on complex targets). The critical caveat OpenAI underlines is in the benchmark name: CyberGym measures reproduction, not novel-zero-day discovery in production systems — a calibration that matters for the policy debate now active in DC. The strategic positioning is deliberate: GPT‑5.5‑Cyber lands as a cybersecurity-tier model with the export-control posture Mythos lost, narrowing the policy fight on Anthropic while picking up the offensive-research demand that Mythos served until 12 June.

OpenAI × Trail of BitsPatch the Planet: 19 projects, 64 PRs, 37 patches already merged in the first week

22 June 2026 · Primary sources: OpenAI index page; Trail of Bits blog

Sitting on top of GPT‑5.5‑Cyber is Patch the Planet, an OpenAI×Trail of Bits programme launched the same day for moving open-source maintainers from security findings to merged fixes. The pipeline is deliberately AI-assisted but human-arbitrated: GPT‑5.5‑Cyber's Codex Security surface generates candidate findings; Trail of Bits engineers manually review every issue, reproduce evidence, dedupe, reassess severity, and submit fixes in accordance with each project's preferences; maintainers retain final authority over what merges and how disclosure happens. HackerOne is in the loop on triage. First-week reported numbers from OpenAI's announcement post: hundreds of bugs surfaced, 64 pull requests filed, 51 issues opened across 19 open-source projects, with 37 patches already merged. The interesting asymmetry: this is the same posture Anthropic argued Mythos was best-in-class for (defensive uplift via offensive-side capability), but with OpenAI's blessing from the Commerce Department on the export-control surface that Anthropic just lost. For maintainers, this is the first credibly-funded, frontier-model-backed analogue to long-running programmes like Google Project Zero, but specifically aimed at throughput on the bottom-of-the-funnel CVE backlog rather than novel cutting-edge research.

AI ResearchAgileThinker: the first credible architecture answer to live-environment agents under time pressure

arXiv 2511.04898 · Mid-June 2026 community read-up · Authors: Tsinghua, Shanghai Jiao Tong, Georgia Tech, Stanford

The paper getting the most weight in the agent-architecture community over the last 48 hours is AgileThinker (arXiv 2511.04898), out of a Tsinghua / Shanghai Jiao Tong / Georgia Tech / Stanford collaboration. The setup it formalises is real-time reasoning: the world keeps changing while the agent is operating — trading desks, live ops monitoring, robot loops, real-time customer support. The empirical claim, summarised in Build This Now's June research round-up: single-paradigm agents fail to stay both correct and on time as pressure rises; AgileThinker, which runs a cheap reactive head and a deeper planner concurrently, is the only configuration that holds correctness as time pressure increases. The framing reframes the standard latency-vs-depth tradeoff as a deliberate architecture choice rather than a hyperparameter knob, and is being read as the agent-research analogue of the Mixture-of-Depths result from late 2025. A companion benchmark drawing attention this week is AgentHallu (693 multi-step agent trajectories across 5 domains and 7 frameworks), which goes after the step-level attribution problem: which specific step in a long-horizon run produced the hallucination, and why. Together the two papers anchor a "real-time + attribution" research pair that competing frontier-lab agent teams will likely benchmark against through Q3.
