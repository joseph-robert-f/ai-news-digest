Six days after the global shutdown, Anthropic restored Fable 5 access on Thursday 18 June — but the model that came back is not the one that went offline. Three things changed under the hood: (i) a new set of auxiliary safety classifiers sits in front of the model, scanning every prompt and every intermediate trajectory for jailbreak patterns and CBRN/cyber-uplift signals, and short-circuiting the trace to a refusal when triggered; (ii) nationality-based access controls are now enforced at the API-onboarding layer, with enhanced compliance screening required before a workspace can call Fable 5 endpoints (per the new Azure Foundry availability documentation); and (iii) the model now falls back to Claude Opus 4.8 more aggressively, especially on cybersecurity, chemistry and biology prompts — Anthropic has not published an updated fallback-rate disclosure, but developers monitoring the picker behaviour in the Anthropic Console and the Claude Android app's coding interface report a measurable shift. Mythos 5 remains restricted to closed-partner cybersecurity work. The legal scaffolding around the restoration is the same Commerce Department directive that triggered the shutdown — still not withdrawn — layered with a new data-retention obligation for Fable 5 usage logs. The practical guidance for teams that re-pinned to Opus 4.8 or GPT-5.5 last week: the rollback path is open, but the refusal surface on Fable 5 is now meaningfully wider than at 9 June GA, so re-validate prompts before reverting any production agents.

Security — Agentjacking

Tenet Security"Agentjacking": one public Sentry DSN hijacks Claude Code, Cursor and Codex with an 85% success rate; 2,388 organisations exposed

12 June disclosure, 17 June validation cut-off, weekend reporting wave 20–22 June · Primary sources: Tenet Security blog, The New Stack, Infosecurity Magazine, Pinggy, GBHackers

Tenet Security researchers Ron Bobrov, Barak Sternberg and Nevo Poran published the full "Agentjacking" disclosure on 12 June, with a validation window that ended 17 June and a weekend reporting wave through 22 June. The attack: an outside party injects fake error events into a target organisation's Sentry project using only a public DSN credential; the Sentry MCP server then surfaces those events to the developer's AI coding agent (Claude Code, Cursor, or OpenAI Codex) as if they were real bug reports to triage; the embedded instructions in the fake error payload are interpreted by the agent as part of the debugging workflow and executed under the developer's full IAM, source, and CI/CD authority. Reported metrics: 85% hijack success rate across the three target agents; 2,388 organisations with exposed Sentry DSNs identified during the validation period, including a Fortune 500 enterprise with a $250 B+ parent company, a $2 B+ hosting infrastructure provider, scientific-computing firms and early-stage startups across six continents. Tenet labels the underlying class of vulnerability the "Authorized Intent Chain": every action in the kill-chain is technically authorised, so the attack bypasses EDR, WAF, IAM controls, VPN, Cloudflare and firewalls entirely — security tooling built to catch unauthorised behaviour does not fire. Sentry was notified on 3 June and has shipped a content filter for the specific PoC payload string, but has explicitly characterised the broader vulnerability as "not technically defensible" at the ingestion layer, because Sentry cannot distinguish malicious payloads from legitimate error messages that themselves contain code snippets and remediation notes. The practical, today-actionable mitigations for any team running Claude Code, Cursor or Codex against an MCP-connected Sentry: (i) treat the raw text of any tool result as untrusted user input, not as agent-trusted context; (ii) rotate any public Sentry DSN that has appeared in client-side JavaScript bundles; (iii) hard-gate destructive operations (filesystem writes, shell commands, package installs, secret reads) behind explicit human confirmation in the agent harness, regardless of which tool's output triggered them.

AI Infrastructure — FERC Reshapes Data-Centre Grid Connections

FERCUnanimous show-cause orders to PJM, MISO, SPP, CAISO, ISO-NE, NYISO — reshapes AI data-centre grid interconnection for 200 M Americans

18 June 2026 · Primary sources: FERC.gov news & fact sheets, Engineering News-Record, TechTimes, American Action Forum

In a unanimous 5–0 vote on Wednesday 18 June, the Federal Energy Regulatory Commission issued tailored "show-cause" orders to six regional grid operators — PJM Interconnection, Midcontinent Independent System Operator (MISO), Southwest Power Pool (SPP), California Independent System Operator (CAISO), ISO New England (ISO-NE), and New York Independent System Operator (NYISO) — requiring each to either justify or revise its rules for how large loads, defined as facilities consuming more than 20 MW, connect to the bulk transmission system. The covered territory serves 200 million Americans across more than 30 states and DC. The substantive change: FERC's expected timelines should materially compress the multi-year interconnection queue backlogs for AI data-centre projects, and tee up the creation of new retail large-load rate classes in many states — a structural answer to the question of whether ratepayers or hyperscaler customers bear the cost of new generation and transmission build-out. The legal architecture is deliberate: by issuing region-specific orders narrowly scoped to FERC's jurisdiction rather than a single national rulemaking, the agency maximises legal durability against state-level pushback. The proceeding stems from a October 2025 directive from Energy Secretary Chris Wright to consider reforms ensuring timely interconnection of AI data centres, and it is now the most concrete piece of AI-infrastructure policy in force after the Trump 2 June Executive Order on Promoting Advanced AI Innovation and Security. For frontier-lab capacity planning, the signal is straightforward: the worst-case 5–7 year wait for a multi-GW interconnect in PJM and CAISO is now meaningfully more likely to land closer to 2–3 years, which materially changes the build-vs-buy calculus that drove the Goldman Sachs $7.6 T 2026–31 AI capex projection covered in the 15 June digest.
