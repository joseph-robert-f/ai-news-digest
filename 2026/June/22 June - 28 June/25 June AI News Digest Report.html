Top Story — OpenAI Ships Its First Silicon: Jalapeño

OpenAI × BroadcomJalapeño Intelligence Processor: 9-month tape-out, GPT‑5.3‑Codex‑Spark running in the lab

24 June 2026 · Primary sources: openai.com; Broadcom investor relations; CNBC; VentureBeat; Tom's Hardware

On Wednesday 24 June OpenAI and Broadcom jointly unveiled Jalapeño, branded as OpenAI's first Intelligence Processor and the first AI accelerator in a multi-generation co-developed compute platform. The architectural framing in OpenAI's announcement is unusually direct: Jalapeño is a blank-slate design for modern LLM inference, not a general-purpose accelerator retrofitted from older AI workloads, built around OpenAI's own roadmap of models, kernels, serving systems, and product latency targets. Reticle-sized ASIC; OpenAI says engineering samples are already running ML workloads in the lab at production target frequency and power, with GPT‑5.3‑Codex‑Spark among the first models exercised on it. Greg Brockman told CNBC the design-to-tape-out cycle took only nine months — what Tom's Hardware calls "what may be the fastest ASIC development cycle ever achieved in high-performance semiconductors" — and was materially accelerated by OpenAI's own models inside the EDA loop. Broadcom contributes silicon implementation and Tomahawk networking; Celestica is the manufacturing partner. Initial deployment is end-of-2026 at gigawatt scale on OpenAI infrastructure, expanding across multiple generations. The strategic shape is the same one Google has occupied for a decade with TPUs and that AWS has reached for with Trainium: a frontier-lab×merchant-fabless pairing that captures inference economics off Nvidia's road. The CNBC framing — that 40% of Jalapeño shipments will be purchased by Microsoft — threads it back into the existing Stargate / Azure compute alliance rather than treating it as an Nvidia-replacement story. Builders should read Jalapeño as the first concrete signal that OpenAI is going down the same vertical-integration path Anthropic chose with the AWS Trainium / Tranium-2 partnership, but with a substantially more aggressive ASIC schedule.

Anthropic × SalesforceClaude Tag in Slack: persistent multiplayer AI teammate, 65% of internal code merged from it

23–24 June 2026 · Primary sources: anthropic.com; salesforce.com; VentureBeat; Fortune; TechCrunch

Anthropic launched Claude Tag — a research preview that replaces the legacy Claude-for-Slack app with a persistent AI teammate available across an organisation's Slack workspace. Behaviourally, an employee @-tags Claude into any channel, hands off a task, and Claude breaks it into stages, works through them asynchronously, accumulates context across conversations, threads, channels, and connected enterprise apps, and follows up proactively on stale threads. Anthropic and Salesforce co-announced the deeper integration: Claude can access Slack channels, messages, and files via Slack's MCP server, and reach into Agentforce 360, Data 360, and Commerce 360 for grounded enterprise context. For builders, the practical implication is that the Slack-MCP path is now a first-class, vendor-blessed surface for cross-app agentic work — not just a community-MCP experiment.

23–24 June 2026 · Primary sources: openai.com; Derya Unutmaz on X; Windows Forum; Crypto Briefing

OpenAI published an unusually substantive blog post detailing how GPT‑5 Pro helped Derya Unutmaz, an immunologist at The Jackson Laboratory for Genomic Medicine, recover the mechanism behind a three-year-old experimental result that his lab had not been able to interpret. The setup — spelled out in the OpenAI announcement and confirmed in Unutmaz's own X thread — was a basic but consequential question in immunology: how does glucose availability modulate the differentiation trajectory of T cells across age groups? Unutmaz handed GPT‑5 Pro a 2022 single-cell dataset that had been sitting unpublished after conventional analyses produced no clean explanation. The model identified age-dependent gene-expression patterns and produced novel hypotheses that aligned with decades of prior literature. The headline detail, and the one most cited in the secondary coverage: when Unutmaz asked GPT‑5 Pro to simulate an experiment on T cells targeting lymphoma, it correctly predicted an enhancement in CD8+ T-cell cytotoxicity — before the lab ran the comparable experiment, and on data the model could not have seen on the internet because Unutmaz had not yet published it. Caveats: this is single-author, single-lab, single-dataset, with the validation pathway still going through standard wet-lab confirmation. But it is the first widely-circulated primary-sourced example of a frontier model surfacing a novel mechanism on truly unseen biological data, and lands in the same week as Jumper's move to Anthropic and the broader life-sciences-AI capital flow. Expect this to anchor the next round of FDA / NIH discussion of AI-co-authored hypotheses.

Software Engineering & Developer Security

GitHub security"Orchid" repo-confusion campaign: >100,000 GitHub repos cloned, automation targets AI agents

Continuing through 24–25 June 2026 · Primary sources: Apiiro research blog; Cybernews; Dark Reading; Cyber Daily

The most consequential live software-engineering security story this week is an automated GitHub repository-confusion campaign, attributed in detail by application-security firm Apiiro and independently mapped by a solo researcher publishing as Orchid. The mechanic is deliberately simple and at scale: attackers fork popular legitimate repositories, inject malicious code into the fork (typically a StealC infostealer payload chained behind seemingly benign install steps), and preserve identical READMEs, names, commit histories, and star-padded stats — relying on (a) Google/Bing search divergence and (b) AI coding agents auto-cloning whichever repo the agent's search heuristics surface first. Apiiro's reported scale: more than 100,000 GitHub repositories affected when downstream developers pull cloned-fork dependencies; Orchid's own automated scanner pinned 9,330 directly-malicious primary repos out of a ~40,000-repo candidate set, of which ~10,000 match the exact malware-distribution signature. The most important reading for builders: this is the first openly-documented campaign that explicitly targets AI agents as the propagation vector, exploiting the fact that an agent acting on "clone the official X repo" will reach for whatever matches the description rather than verifying provenance. Mitigation now showing up in vendor playbooks: pin clone URLs by exact org/owner, require maintainer GPG signatures, and configure agentic IDEs to flag star/age/commit-graph anomalies on cold-pulled dependencies. GitHub's own response is still expanding; expect a platform-level provenance signal in the next Trust & Safety drop.

EU AI ActThe Digital Omnibus pushes the Annex III high-risk deadline from August 2026 to December 2027 — GPAI window still hits 2 August

As of 25 June 2026 · Primary sources: artificialintelligenceact.eu; AI Act Service Desk (EC); Council of the EU; Ogletree analysis

The most concrete near-term policy timeline shifting this week is the EU AI Act enforcement calendar. Under the Digital Omnibus provisional agreement reached on 7 May 2026, the high-risk-system rules in Annex III are deferred from 2 August 2026 to 2 December 2027. What remains live as of today: prohibited practices have been enforceable since 2 February 2025; the GPAI-model rules became applicable on 2 August 2025; and the broad enforcement crossover — including Article 50 transparency rules — is still scheduled for 2 August 2026, just 38 days away. Providers of GPAI models already on the market before the cutover have until 2 August 2027 to comply. The relevant cross-reference with today's news: yesterday's Five Eyes joint advisory (24 June digest) and the 2 June White House Executive Order on Promoting Advanced Artificial Intelligence Innovation and Security (which explicitly preserves an "AI cybersecurity clearinghouse", a voluntary frontier-model evaluation framework, and bars mandatory governmental licensing or pre-clearance of model releases) together set the US/UK/EU triangle: a deliberately voluntary US frontier evaluation surface, a hardening mandatory EU evaluation surface, and a Five Eyes intelligence-coordination layer running orthogonally to both. For US-based labs targeting the EU market, the Annex III delay materially reduces compliance pressure on most high-risk vertical deployments through 2027 — but does not push the GPAI obligations or transparency clock.
