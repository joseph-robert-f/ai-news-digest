AnthropicWhen AI builds itself: Jack Clark calls for a global pause as Claude now writes 80%+ of Anthropic's merged code

4–5 June 2026 · Primary source: Anthropic Institute paper · Axios / Scientific American / CNN Business / Fortune

The newly stood-up Anthropic Institute published "When AI builds itself" on Thursday 4 June — an unusually candid position paper from co-founder and policy lead Jack Clark that proposes a coordinated, opt-in global pause on frontier development the moment any lab crosses into recursive self-improvement (RSI): an AI system that can autonomously design, train, and deploy its own successor with minimal human input. Anthropic's framing of the threshold is concrete — the pause would need agreement from multiple well-resourced labs, mutually agreed trigger conditions, and a dedicated oversight body to administer it. The data underneath the policy ask is the most-cited number of the week: more than 80% of code merged into Anthropic's own production codebase in May was authored by Claude, up from low single digits when Claude Code launched in February 2025; the typical Anthropic engineer is now merging roughly 8× the daily lines of code they did in 2024; and Claude's success rate on the hardest open-ended SWE-bench-style problems has climbed to 76% in May 2026, a 50-point jump in six months. Clark argues the timeline to a system that can self-improve without humans in the loop is "plausibly within two years." Critics, including WION and Tom's Hardware, have noted the obvious tension — Anthropic is requesting a coordinated brake on the very capability profile its IPO prospectus will sell against — and a Claude-generated rebuttal circulating on X ("the pause button has no cords") has been read as deliberate pre-IPO theatre. Whatever the motive, this is the most explicit RSI policy ask from a frontier lab to date.

White HouseExecutive Order on Advanced AI Innovation and Security: federal AI Cybersecurity Clearinghouse, voluntary frontier framework

2 June 2026 · Primary source: whitehouse.gov / Covington Inside Privacy / Tenable

President Trump signed a new Executive Order on Promoting Advanced Artificial Intelligence Innovation and Security on Monday 2 June. The order takes a two-track posture: (1) hardening U.S. government and critical-infrastructure cyber defences against advanced AI used adversarially, and (2) standing up a voluntary benchmarking and review framework for the secure development and release of frontier models. The single biggest operational change is the creation, within 30 days, of an AI Cybersecurity Clearinghouse co-led by Treasury, the National Cyber Director, NSA (via the Secretary of War) and CISA, in voluntary collaboration with frontier labs and critical-infrastructure operators — tasked with coordinating vulnerability scanning, validation, patch distribution, and deconflicting agent-driven security research. Critically, the order expressly forbids establishment of a mandatory licensing, pre-clearance, or permitting regime for AI models — locking U.S. policy into a deliberately lighter-touch posture than the EU AI Act timeline. The clearinghouse is the U.S. policy mirror of Anthropic's Project Glasswing (private-sector consortium) and OpenAI's parallel EU Cyber Action Plan announced last week. Federal contractors should expect downstream NIST SP 800-218A and FedRAMP updates that codify the voluntary-attestation pathway into procurement language by Q4.

OpenAIGPT-5.5-Cyber lands in the EU under a new Cyber Action Plan; ENISA and EU AI Office gain access

1–5 June 2026 · Primary source: OpenAI / eWeek / TechTimes

OpenAI activated its EU Cyber Action Plan on Monday 1 June, opening limited-preview access to GPT-5.5-Cyber — the cybersecurity-tuned variant of GPT-5.5 — to vetted European defenders, businesses, governments, national cyber agencies and EU institutions including the EU AI Office. GPT-5.5-Cyber is not a more powerful base model than GPT-5.5; the differentiator is policy: the model is permitted to do vulnerability identification, malware analysis, reverse engineering, and patch validation work that the general-purpose GPT-5.5 declines. The activation comes with a hard security requirement — from 1 June, individuals using the cyber model must enable Advanced Account Security with phishing-resistant protections, and organisations must attest to phishing-resistant SSO. The signal is the deliberate policy asymmetry with Anthropic: OpenAI is willing to open its restricted cyber model to a sovereign regulator (ENISA, EU AI Office), while Anthropic still keeps the more capable Claude Mythos entirely closed-partner. The UK AI Security Institute's benchmark from the same window helps explain the divergence: in a 32-step simulated corporate cyberattack chain, GPT-5.5-Cyber completed it end-to-end 2 of 10 times, while Claude Mythos completed it 3 of 10 times.
