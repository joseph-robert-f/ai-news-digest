Frontier Model & Product Releases

OpenAIDreaming V3 memory begins rolling out to ChatGPT Plus and Pro — with 5× compute cut clearing path to free tier

4–5 June 2026 · Primary source: OpenAI / gHacks / Implicator

OpenAI began the staged rollout of Dreaming V3, a new hierarchical memory architecture for ChatGPT, to Plus and Pro users in the US starting Thursday. The change replaces the previous "saved memories" list as the primary store: a background consolidation process — deliberately framed as analogous to how the human brain processes memory during sleep — periodically synthesises an implicit global memory from accumulated conversation, with the user-facing list now functioning as a transparency surface rather than the source of truth. Users get a new memory-summary page with controls to inspect, edit, add, or correct retained information — addressing one of the persistent complaints about ChatGPT's previous memory implementation, namely that users could not see why the model remembered them a certain way. The most strategically interesting line in the rollout post is the engineering claim: OpenAI says recent efficiency work cut the compute needed to serve Dreaming by approximately 5×, enabling expansion to free-tier users in the coming weeks. Coming the same week as Microsoft's MAI announcement, the timing matters — OpenAI is moving consumer features that genuinely require a long-term memory substrate (cross-session personalisation, agent handoff) into the path of free-tier reach before MAI-powered Copilot can credibly compete on the same axis.

Policy & Regulation

US PolicyHouse discussion draft: "Great American Artificial Intelligence Act" — 3-year preemption of state AI model laws, mandatory third-party audits for >$500 M revenue developers

4 June 2026 · Primary source: Rep. Trahan section-by-section / Axios / Roll Call

Reps. Jay Obernolte (R-CA) and Lori Trahan (D-MA) released a 269-page bipartisan discussion draft on Wednesday creating a national AI framework, named the Great American Artificial Intelligence Act. The two structurally significant provisions: (1) the bill preempts state laws specifically regulating AI model development for three years (state laws on AI use and deployment are explicitly preserved, a deliberate carveout designed to keep blue-state consumer-protection AI laws intact); and (2) frontier developers with >$500 M in annual revenue must publish safety frameworks, file critical-incident reports, and submit to semi-annual third-party audits. It codifies the Commerce Department's Center for AI Standards and Innovation (CAISI) — the successor to AISI — and creates an AI Workforce Research Hub. The draft lands days after President Trump's executive order on voluntary federal AI model reviews, and within hours drew opposition from unions (AFA-CWA, AFT) and from AI-safety advocates including a sharp letter from Center for AI Safety calling the preemption clause "a generational mistake." The Senate companion is reportedly being shopped to Senators Heinrich, Lummis, and Warner; markup is not expected before recess. For product teams, the operative number is the $500 M revenue threshold — it is now the de-facto definitional line for what counts as a "frontier developer" in US federal law, and aligns with parallel UK AISI thresholds but is materially lower than the EU AI Act's GPAI threshold.

Research & Alignment

DeepMind"Solipsistic Superintelligence is Unlikely to be Cooperative" — DeepMind position paper on the train–deploy gap

4 June 2026 · Primary source: arXiv 2606.03237

Google DeepMind posted a position-style paper, Solipsistic Superintelligence is Unlikely to be Cooperative, to arXiv on Thursday. The authors argue that an AI system designed as a single-agent task-solver — treating the world as static and the deployment context as just another distribution to optimise over — is structurally unlikely to behave cooperatively once deployed. The mechanism is what the paper calls endogenous non-stationarity: the deployment of a sufficiently capable agent itself shifts the distribution it operates in (other agents adapt, infrastructure changes, the principal's goals evolve), so the training distribution is mechanically misaligned with the deployment distribution in a way that no static dataset can fix. The paper's call to action is a non-solipsistic research paradigm: treat interdependence with other agents and with the principal as a first-class design constraint, rather than treating cooperation as another reward to optimise after the fact. The contribution is mostly conceptual but it is doing real work inside DeepMind's published alignment agenda — it provides a theoretical framing for why long-horizon agentic systems (the same systems DeepMind is shipping commercially via Gemini's agent surfaces) require a different research approach than next-token loss reduction. Reads as a deliberate counterweight to the more capability-celebratory Hassabis "foothills of the singularity" framing covered last week.
