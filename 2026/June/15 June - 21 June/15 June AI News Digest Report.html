Jassy then escalated to senior administration officials directly. Several phone calls between Anthropic CEO Dario Amodei and the administration followed; Amodei argued the bypass was narrow and reproducible on other publicly available models. The administration disagreed. The result was Commerce Secretary Howard Lutnick's 5:21 PM ET Friday letter ordering the suspension of foreign-national access. The conflict-of-interest backdrop here is genuinely strange: Amazon is Anthropic's largest investor — cumulative ~$13 B in equity, plus a $100 B AWS Trainium/Inferentia spending commitment over a multi-year horizon, plus the Project Rainier compute build-out (covered in the 27 May digest as part of the Fujitsu-Anthropic alliance write-up). Amazon is also the cloud host of Mythos-class deployments via Bedrock. That makes Jassy's call simultaneously a fiduciary act (protect the investor's downside from a security mishap), a competitive act (raise the cost of Anthropic's standalone product motion versus Amazon's own Nova roadmap), and a regulatory act (light the fuse the White House detonated). For the developer ecosystem, the takeaway is concrete: your supplier's largest backer is now also functionally a regulator of that supplier, and the equilibrium that produces is unfamiliar.

Security ResearchPliny the Liberator's 120,040-character Fable 5 system prompt leak, dissected: a de-facto field manual for long-running agents

10 June leak · Weekend 13–15 June community dissection wave · Primary sources: CL4R1T4S GitHub repo, jailbreak-fable repo, alphasignalai, Cybersecurity News

Five days into the Fable 5 GA, on 10 June, the red-teamer who publishes under Pliny the Liberator uploaded what he says is the complete Fable 5 system prompt — 120,040 characters — to his public CL4R1T4S GitHub repository, along with the multi-agent "Pack Hunt" jailbreak harness in a companion jailbreak-fable repo. Over the weekend the leaked prompt was dissected at length by the agent-tools community, and several findings sharpened: (i) the prompt is explicitly optimised for multi-hour, multi-day autonomous coding sessions rather than single-shot answers, with elaborate state-resumption rituals, scratchpad / TodoWrite conventions, and stop-conditions; (ii) safety guardrails are layered — there is a refusal classifier on top of the model, and the system prompt itself encodes the high-level safety policy; (iii) the prompt instructs the model to "never narrate internal deliberation", instead emitting short, declarative status updates — a directly portable instruction for any agent harness; (iv) it documents tight rules for tool selection, parallelism, and confirmation thresholds for destructive operations. Anthropic, for its part, argues the Pliny demonstration was not a true jailbreak: the model's core safety classifiers were not bypassed, and the outputs surfaced did not provide meaningful uplift toward bioweapons synthesis or advanced cyber-exploit development. The technical-policy point is independent of the political one: even taken at Anthropic's framing, the leak is itself instructive, and dozens of open-source agent harnesses already published over the weekend now treat the leaked prompt as a reference design.

Hugging FaceSerge: a GitHub-native, Apache-2.0 AI code reviewer that runs on any OpenAI-compatible model

Released 12 June 2026 · Adoption wave 13–15 June · Primary source: huggingface/serge GitHub & Hugging Face blog

Hugging Face shipped Serge, a GitHub-native AI code-review system released under Apache 2.0, on Friday 12 June. Serge runs as a GitHub App that posts inline review comments on pull requests in the same surface as a human reviewer, and is designed to be backend-agnostic: it talks to any OpenAI-compatible endpoint, including Hugging Face Inference Providers, vLLM/TGI servers, llama.cpp, Ollama, or a commercial API. Hugging Face says Serge is already in use on its own diffusers, transformers, and Serge-itself repos. The positioning is deliberate: rather than replace human review, Serge is targeted at catching the bottom-of-the-funnel issues — obvious bugs, missing tests, stylistic regressions, and review-comment follow-up — freeing maintainer time for design-level review. It lands directly in the same competitive surface as GitHub Copilot Code Review (now generally available), CodeRabbit, Greptile, and Cursor BugBot, but with two distinct properties: (a) the entire system is open source and self-hostable, and (b) the model swap is a config flag rather than a vendor lock-in. For teams already running local models, Serge is the first credibly-open, GitHub-native review pipeline that does not assume a hosted-frontier-model billing relationship.

South Korea's NAVER — the country's search and cloud incumbent and the developer of the HyperCLOVA X sovereign model family — signed a multi-year, multi-phase commitment to build gigawatt-scale AI factories on NVIDIA's newly unveiled DSX platform. NAVER is the first big-name sovereign-AI customer to commit publicly to the DSX stack — relevant given the Korean government's broader sovereign-AI push and the ongoing equipment-and-power debate around the AI build-out (covered separately in the Goldman piece below).

European CommissionCode of Practice on marking and labelling AI-generated content — binding transparency rules take effect 2 August

Published 10 June 2026 · Primary source: European Commission / digital-strategy.ec.europa.eu

The European Commission published its Code of Practice on the marking and labelling of AI-generated content on 10 June 2026, the operational guidance document that anchors the EU AI Act's Article 50 transparency obligations coming into force on 2 August 2026. The Code lays out the expected machine-readable provenance metadata (C2PA-aligned), the visible disclosure requirements for deepfakes and synthetic media, and the carve-outs for clearly artistic and satirical content. Signatories will be presumed compliant on the labelling subset; non-signatories must demonstrate equivalent measures. The Code lands in the context of the broader AI Act Omnibus Agreement reached on 13 May, which deferred most high-risk system obligations (Annex III stand-alone) to 2 December 2027, and Annex I product-embedded systems to 2 August 2028 — but explicitly did not defer the transparency surface, which remains on the August 2026 timeline. The 2 August deadline is now the next concrete EU compliance forcing function for any model provider with significant European exposure.
