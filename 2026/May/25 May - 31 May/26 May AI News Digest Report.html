Top Story — Software Supply Chain

SecurityThe Nx Console breach: how 18 minutes on the VS Code Marketplace cost GitHub 3,800 internal repos — and burned OpenAI, Grafana & Mistral too

18–25 May 2026 · Primary source: GitHub CISO statement / Nx postmortem / Grafana Labs blog

The picture coming into focus over the long weekend is the most consequential developer-supply-chain incident of 2026 so far. On 11 May, threat group TeamPCP (also tracked as UNC6780) compromised 42 TanStack npm packages, publishing 84 malicious versions containing a credential stealer. Those stolen credentials let them impersonate a legitimate Nx maintainer and, on Monday 18 May at 12:30 UTC, push a poisoned build of Nx Console v18.95.0 (nrwl.angular-console) to the Visual Studio Marketplace. The malicious build was live for just 18 minutes before takedown at 12:48 UTC — long enough to ship to thousands of developer machines. Within seconds of any workspace opening, the extension fetched a 498 KB obfuscated payload from a dangling orphan commit hidden inside the official nrwl/nx repo and harvested 1Password vaults, Anthropic Claude Code configs (~/.claude/settings.json), npm, GitHub, and AWS credentials — the first publicly documented supply-chain payload to specifically target AI-coding-assistant secrets.

On 19 May, GitHub CISO Alexis Wales publicly disclosed that an employee device was compromised and that the attacker exfiltrated approximately 3,800 GitHub-internal repositories, calling the attacker's number "directionally consistent" with GitHub's own forensics. Customer-owned repos were not impacted. CVE-2026-48027 was assigned. By 21 May, downstream casualties were confirmed: Grafana Labs said a missed token led to attackers reaching its private GitHub org; OpenAI disclosed two compromised employee devices (no production or IP impact); Mistral AI is also on the confirmed-victim list. TeamPCP and LAPSUS$ have since teamed up on a joint $95,000 forum sale of the stolen GitHub data. The propagation mechanism — an automated worm TeamPCP calls Mini Shai-Hulud — turns stolen CI/CD credentials into new poisoned npm releases, which is the loop that fed Nx Console in the first place.

Frontier Research

DeepMindAlphaProof Nexus: an LLM + Lean system closes 9 Erdős problems and 44 OEIS conjectures, with proofs you can re-check at home

21–22 May 2026 · Primary source: arXiv 2605.22763 / google-deepmind/alphaproof-nexus-results

Google DeepMind published Advancing Mathematics Research with AI-Driven Formal Proof Search on arXiv on 21 May, alongside the public release of the alphaproof-nexus-results GitHub repo. AlphaProof Nexus is the successor to the IMO-era AlphaProof, and the architecture is the same family but scaled out: a frontier language model (Gemini 3.1 Pro) proposes proof steps in Lean 4, and the Lean kernel mechanically verifies every step — eliminating the hallucination problem that plagues "natural language" math results from LLMs. The system autonomously closed nine open Erdős problems — including two that had been open for 56 years — and produced fully formal proofs of 44 open conjectures from the OEIS (the Online Encyclopedia of Integer Sequences). Per-problem compute cost was on the order of "a few hundred dollars."

The result is methodologically distinct from OpenAI's recent unit-distance disproof: OpenAI's was a single new construction by a general reasoning model with no formal verifier in the loop, whereas AlphaProof Nexus is a system — LLM proposer + Lean verifier — producing machine-checked proofs in additive combinatorics, algebraic geometry, graph theory, optimization, and quantum optics. Both shots landed inside a single week, and together they mark the first time two frontier labs have independently demonstrated AI systems solving research-grade open problems. DeepMind has open-sourced every Lean proof, which means the community can audit (and the next system can train on) the results directly.

The headline number from SpaceX's IPO prospectus, filed last week, isn't about Starlink — it's about Anthropic. Per the S-1 disclosure, Anthropic has signed a multi-year cloud-services agreement with SpaceX / xAI (the two were merged earlier this year into "SpaceXAI") under which Anthropic will pay $1.25 billion every month from July 2026 through May 2029 for the entire compute output of Colossus 1, xAI's Memphis-area campus. At full ramp, that is roughly $15 B in annualized revenue to SpaceX/xAI, and an aggregate ~$45 B over the life of the contract. Anthropic separately confirmed in xAI's press release that the contract also includes an "expression of interest" in jointly developing multi-gigawatt compute capacity in space with SpaceX — the first formal mention of orbital data-center work tied to a frontier model lab.

The deal materially closes the gap between Anthropic's reported run-rate revenue (~$30 B run-rate as of late May, up from ~$9 B at year-end 2025) and its compute bill: under the contract, Anthropic alone funds well over a third of xAI's projected 2027 revenue. It also explains an oddity of the past month's competing IPO calendars — SpaceX, OpenAI, and Anthropic are now operationally entangled in ways that complicate each filing's risk factors, because every lab's compute supply now depends on a counterparty whose own valuation depends on AI demand.

White HouseTrump pulls AI executive order at the last minute; the 90-day government pre-release preview is now in limbo

21 May 2026 · Primary source: Oval Office press gaggle / Axios / Washington Post

President Trump postponed the signing ceremony for an AI-industry executive order that had been on the calendar for last Thursday afternoon. Asked in the Oval Office why, the President said simply: "I postponed it… I didn't like certain aspects." Reporting from Axios and The Washington Post indicates that AI policy adviser David Sacks opposed the draft, and that the order's signature provision — a back-channel under which the government would preview frontier models for up to 90 days before public release — clashed with the President's stated view that "we're leading China, we're leading everybody, and I didn't want to do anything to get in the way of that lead." Beyond pre-release review, the draft order would have set policy on private-sector collaboration to harden critical infrastructure, on protecting US AI IP from foreign theft, and on accelerating government acquisition of frontier-AI-enabled capabilities.

The optics are awkward for the labs. Microsoft and xAI had already publicly agreed to give regulators early access for testing under a parallel voluntary framework. With the EO pulled, the only operative pre-release pathway is now the UK AI Safety Institute and the EU AI Office under the GPAI Code of Practice, whose transparency rules take effect 2 August 2026. The order's fate is unclear; the White House has not committed to a new signing date.

AnthropicMCP tunnels and self-hosted sandboxes for Claude Managed Agents enter customer environments

At Code with Claude London on 19 May — Anthropic's first developer conference in Europe — the company announced two pieces of plumbing that move Claude Managed Agents inside the enterprise perimeter. It also positions MCP — the protocol Anthropic open-sourced in late 2024 — as the de facto transport for agent tooling at the enterprise tier, which matters as Google's competing WebMCP origin trial heads into Chrome 149.

LinuxLinux 7.0 ships: Rust loses its "experimental" label, and 80% of kernel CVEs are now on the chopping block

23–25 May 2026 · Primary source: Phoronix / kernel.org / RustWeek 2026

Linux 7.0 shipped over the weekend with the most consequential change in its language profile in three decades: Rust is no longer marked "experimental" in the kernel and is now officially supported on the same footing as C. The release also lands XFS self-healing, post-quantum primitives upstream, faster EXT4 concurrent direct-I/O writes, and ARM64 / RISC-V performance work. At RustWeek 2026 in Utrecht the same week, kernel maintainer Greg Kroah-Hartman sketched a Rust-based proposal — still in development — that he estimates would eliminate roughly 80% of the CVEs the Linux kernel currently generates: about 60% comes from two compile-time-checkable bug classes alone (forgetting to check error return values, and forgetting to release locks), both of which Rust catches statically. For software engineering at the systems tier, this is the inflection point at which Rust stops being a side-project in the kernel tree and starts being the language drivers and security-sensitive subsystems are expected to be written in by default.
