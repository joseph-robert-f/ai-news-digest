AnthropicClaude Opus 4.8 ships — mid-turn system messages, effort dial, dynamic workflows, 3× cheaper Fast mode

28 May 2026 · Primary source: Anthropic Newsroom / AWS / Simon Willison

Anthropic released Claude Opus 4.8 on 28 May, day-of with the funding close. The company describes the model as a "modest but tangible improvement" over Opus 4.7. Top benchmark deltas: 88.6% on SWE-bench Verified, 69.2% on SWE-bench Pro, and 1890 on GDPval-AA (~121 Elo above GPT-5.5). Anthropic claims Opus 4.8 is 4× less likely to introduce flaws into the code it writes than Opus 4.7, and it has been trained to flag uncertainty rather than confabulate when it cannot verify a claim. New product surface: (1) mid-conversation system messages, allowing a fresh role: "system" turn after a user turn without restating the full prompt; (2) an effort dial letting callers trade latency and cost for additional thinking on a per-call basis; (3) dynamic workflows in Claude Code research preview, in which the model decomposes long-horizon tasks into sub-agents on its own. Standard pricing holds at $5 / $25 per million input/output tokens; Fast mode is now 3× cheaper than on prior Opus generations. Opus 4.8 is GA across the Anthropic API, Bedrock, Vertex, AWS Trainium and Inferentia. Anthropic separately reiterated that Mythos-class models are days–weeks from a public rollout, following expansion of the Project Glasswing defensive-security partner program.

AI Safety, Policy & Red-Teaming

OpenAIFrontier Governance Framework published — OpenAI's public answer to California SB-53 and the EU AI Act

29 May 2026 · Primary source: OpenAI

This morning OpenAI published its Frontier Governance Framework (FGF), a public-facing governance document that sits alongside (and operationalizes) its internal Preparedness Framework. It is explicitly written to map onto two emerging legal regimes: California's Transparency in Frontier AI Act (SB-53) and the EU AI Act's Code of Practice for General Purpose AI. The FGF covers risk assessment and mitigation across cyber-offense, CBRN, harmful manipulation, and loss of control; it specifies model-reporting cadence, security risk management, incident response, external expert input, and a versioned framework-update process. The published document is the first in what OpenAI says will be a continuously evolving series: future versions will track new evaluations, capability thresholds, and regulatory text as Sacramento and Brussels finalize obligations. The framework drops seven days after OpenAI's confidential S-1 filing and four weeks ahead of the expected commencement of SB-53's reporting obligations, lining up disclosure to regulators with disclosure to public-markets investors.

Cisco AI Defense15-model, ~7,000-conversation red team: no closed frontier model is immune to multi-turn attacks

27–28 May 2026 · Primary source: Cisco Blogs / Cisco AI Defense

Cisco's AI threat-intelligence team released a benchmark study covering 15 closed flagship models from OpenAI, Anthropic, Google, Amazon and xAI, comparing ~30,000 single-turn prompts against ~7,000 multi-turn attacks spread across more than 1,400 conversations. Headline result: single-turn attack success rates (ASR) of 2.19–64.91% balloon to 7.89–88.30% under iterative, conversational pressure — an order-of-magnitude gap that is essentially invisible in vendor-published safety scores. Anthropic's Claude family posted the strongest defense (single-turn 2.19–3.64%, multi-turn 11.16–16.20%). Gemini 3 Pro went from ~18% single-turn to 73% multi-turn. Grok 4.1 Fast (non-reasoning) topped the cohort at 88.30%, but the same model dropped to 43.5% once reasoning mode was switched on — a swing Cisco says is not captured by any public benchmark or model card it reviewed. The paper argues that industry-standard single-prompt evals systematically misrank frontier models, and pushes for multi-turn ASR as a required disclosure in framework documents like the one OpenAI shipped this morning.

OpenAICodex + Thrive: a self-improving tax agent that reaches 97% field-level accuracy in six weeks

27–28 May 2026 · Primary source: OpenAI

OpenAI and Thrive Holdings published a joint engineering write-up describing a production agent built on Codex that prepares U.S. tax returns and self-improves from accountant feedback. The system ingests practitioner corrections, traces of prior filings, and a structured evaluation harness, and then uses Codex to run targeted code modifications against the agent itself in response to recurring failure modes. Reported deployment numbers: 7,000+ returns processed across 30+ accounting firms, ~one-third reduction in prep time, ~50% throughput uplift, and up to 97% field-level accuracy. The capability curve is the most striking part: at launch only 25% of returns hit 75% correct field completion; six weeks later, 86% did. Boris Power led the OpenAI side of the build; Thrive owns the resulting IP. Strategically, the post is OpenAI's first public, end-to-end demonstration that Codex is now an automation surface for capability-improving feedback loops — not just code suggestion — and it gives the freshly launched OpenAI Deployment Company (DeployCo) a flagship reference deployment.

YouTubeAuto-labels for photorealistic AI video — whether the creator discloses it or not

27 May 2026 · Primary source: YouTube Blog

YouTube announced a structural overhaul of its AI-content labels. If a creator does not voluntarily disclose AI use, YouTube's internal detection signals will now automatically apply a label when its systems identify "significant photorealistic AI use." The label format has also been simplified to a single label for all photorealistic and meaningfully AI-altered content. For long-form video the label moves directly below the player and above the description (instead of inside the description), making it visible without expanding the metadata. On Shorts the label becomes a player overlay. Two categories of label are permanent and non-removable: content created with YouTube's own AI tools (Veo, Dream Screen), and content whose C2PA metadata indicates it was fully AI-generated — the first explicit policy on the platform treating C2PA as authoritative for labeling purposes. YouTube says the label has no impact on recommendations or monetization. The change lands the same week as YouTube's broader push to update creators on agentic discovery and AI-overview integration with Search.
