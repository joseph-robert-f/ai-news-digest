MicrosoftMay Patch Tuesday: ~120 CVEs, 16 surfaced by an AI bug-finder ("MDASH"), hotpatching on by default

13 May 2026 · Primary source: MSRC Security Update Guide

Microsoft shipped its May 2026 security rollup yesterday, addressing roughly 120 vulnerabilities (17 rated Critical) across Windows, Office, Azure, Dynamics 365, and the .NET stack — and, for the first time since June 2024, with zero actively-exploited zero-days. Standouts: CVE-2026-41096 (CVSS 9.8 RCE in Windows DNS Client), CVE-2026-41089 (CVSS 9.8 Netlogon stack overflow), and CVE-2026-42898 (CVSS 9.9 in on-prem Dynamics 365). Microsoft disclosed that 16 of the fixed flaws were discovered by MDASH — its internal "multi-model agentic scanning harness" — and warned that AI-assisted discovery will materially increase Patch Tuesday volume in coming months. The same release flips hotpatching on by default for eligible Windows 11 devices and adds "Agents on the Taskbar", letting Windows treat first- and third-party AI agents as taskbar-resident apps with MDM controls.

Google TIGFirst confirmed AI-developed zero-day caught mid-campaign: 2FA-bypass against a popular sysadmin tool

11–12 May 2026 · Primary source: Google Cloud Threat Intelligence Blog

Google's Threat Intelligence Group (GTIG) published the first incident report it considers high-confidence evidence that an LLM was used to discover and weaponize a true zero-day. A financially-motivated actor had partnered with prominent eCrime affiliates to stage a mass exploitation event against a widely-deployed open-source web-based sysadmin tool, using a Python implant that bypasses 2FA. The exploit's tells — verbose educational docstrings, a hallucinated CVSS score, and textbook PEP-8-style structure — led GTIG to conclude AI assistance with high confidence. Google worked with the vendor to disclose and patch before the campaign launched. The same write-up flags APT45 (DPRK) using AI to validate thousands of exploit candidates and Chinese state actors experimenting with LLM-driven initial-access pipelines. This is the inflection many CISO teams have been pricing in: AI-assisted offensive ops are no longer hypothetical.

13 May 2026 · Primary source: Adaption Labs

Adaption — the San Francisco lab founded by former Cohere VP of Research Sara Hooker and former Cohere director of inference Sudip Roy — emerged with AutoScientist, a system that co-optimizes data mixture and training recipe in a single closed loop and runs the loop autonomously until quality converges on a user-defined objective. The bet is that the next frontier is not larger models but smarter, faster fine-tuning: the team claims AutoScientist more than doubles win rates across the base models they tested and compresses what is usually a multi-week alignment cycle into an afternoon. Adaption is making the tool free for 30 days. If the result holds, this is one of the first credible public attempts to put a recognizable slice of frontier-lab tooling — the part labs guard most jealously — in third-party hands.

Google ResearchTurboQuant (ICLR 2026): 3-bit KV cache with no accuracy loss; community ports land in llama.cpp / vLLM

12–14 May 2026 · Primary source: Google Research Blog / ICLR 2026

Google Research's TurboQuant — presented at ICLR 2026 and now circulating widely in serving stacks — is a data-oblivious two-stage vector quantization scheme (PolarQuant rotation + Lloyd-Max scalar quantization, with a 1-bit QJL residual correction) that compresses transformer KV caches to as little as 3 bits per coordinate with provably near-optimal unbiased inner-product estimation and no measurable downstream accuracy loss. Memory footprint drops by roughly 6×, and because the rotation and quantization are cheaper than the memory-bandwidth they save, inference is actually faster on Gemma and Mistral baselines — no fine-tuning, no calibration, no model-specific tweaks. Independent reimplementations landed this week as a llama.cpp discussion and an open-source PyTorch/Triton port targeting vLLM, accelerating real-world adoption.
