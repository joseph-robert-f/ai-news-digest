Google DeepMind"AI Co-Mathematician" sets new FrontierMath Tier 4 SOTA at 48%

7 May 2026 · Primary source: arXiv 2605.06651 / DeepMind blog

Google DeepMind released AI Co-Mathematician, a stateful, asynchronous, multi-agent workbench built on Gemini Deep Think for open-ended mathematical research. A "project coordinator" delegates to specialized sub-agents (literature search, theorem proving, code execution, reviewer) running in parallel, tracking failed hypotheses and producing LaTeX write-ups with margin annotations. On FrontierMath Tier 4 — the hardest, non-public set of problems — the system solved 23 of 48 problems (48%), well above GPT-5.5 Pro at 39.6% and Claude Opus 4.7 at 22.9%, and more than 2× the underlying Gemini 3.1 Pro base (19%). The paper highlights a real research win from Oxford's Marc Lackenby, who used the system to resolve an open Kourovka Notebook problem in group theory after a reviewer-agent caught a flaw in the AI's first proof attempt.

AnthropicClaude Managed Agents now "Dream": offline memory consolidation in research preview

6–7 May 2026 · Primary source: Claude API docs / Anthropic

Anthropic put Dreams into research preview as part of an update to Claude Managed Agents. Dreams is a scheduled, between-sessions process that reads an agent's prior session transcripts and memory store, extracts cross-session patterns (recurring failures, converged workflows, shared preferences across a team of agents), and emits a new, reorganized memory store with duplicates merged and stale entries replaced. Anthropic positions it as one rung above conventional vector-store memory, more analogous to overnight memory consolidation in humans than to retrieval-augmented generation. Harvey (legal-AI) reported ~6× higher task-completion rates after enabling Dreams in early access. Dreams currently ships only via Anthropic's developer access program, alongside two other Managed Agents updates rolled out the same window.

European Union"Digital Omnibus on AI" deal pushes high-risk AI Act obligations to December 2027

7 May 2026 · Primary source: Council of the EU

The Council of the EU and the European Parliament reached a provisional agreement on the "Digital Omnibus on AI", a cleanup package that simplifies parts of the bloc's flagship AI Act and meaningfully delays its bite. The headline change postpones enforcement of obligations on high-risk AI systems — biometrics, critical infrastructure, education, employment, migration, border control — from 2 August 2026 to 2 December 2027, a 16-month delay. The package also adds a mechanism for resolving overlap between the AI Act and sectoral law (machinery, medical devices, etc.), exempting the EU Machinery Regulation from direct applicability of the AI Act. The deal lands after months of public lobbying from Siemens, ASML, Airbus, SAP, Ericsson, Mistral, and Nokia, with Siemens CEO Roland Busch having warned the company would shift AI investment to the U.S. and China otherwise. Civil-society groups responded by accusing Brussels of caving to industry just weeks before the rules were due to apply.
