Python3.15.0 beta 1 ships: feature freeze, lazy imports, frozen dicts, centralized profiling

5 May 2026 · Primary source: Python Insider

The Python core team shipped 3.15.0b1 on Tuesday, locking the 3.15 feature set and starting the four-beta path to RC. Headlining the release: PEP 810 explicit lazy imports, a new frozen-dict built-in, and a centralized profiling package that consolidates the historical zoo of cProfile/profile/perf entry points. The release also lands PEP 803 stabilizing the free-threaded ABI, with the incremental GC reverted (a notable rollback from 3.14). Separately, Python's packaging governance moved this week as well, with the public formation of a Python Packaging Council covered in Real Python's monthly recap.

SecurityCVE-2026-8217: weaponized RCE in IAS Canias ERP 8.03 published 10 May

10 May 2026 · Primary source: NVD / CVE.org

A remote-code-execution flaw in IAS Canias ERP 8.03 — tracked as CVE-2026-8217 — was published today. The bug lives in the Runtime.getRuntime().exec() path of the RMI Interface component: a tainted troiaCode argument allows OS-command injection on the host. A working public exploit has already been released, and CVSS framing puts it in the "patch immediately" tier for any enterprise running Canias-backed manufacturing or finance workflows. Defenders should treat the RMI listener as untrusted, restrict it to internal segments, and audit any wrapper services that pass user input into the affected method. The disclosure is a useful reminder that 2026's most exploited vulnerabilities are still boring command-injection bugs in long-running enterprise stacks — not novel AI-discovered zero-days.

Five EyesFirst-ever joint agentic-AI security guidance: assume agents will misbehave

1–8 May 2026 · Primary source: CISA / NSA / NCSC joint publication

The six Five-Eyes cyber agencies — CISA, NSA, ASD's ACSC, Canadian CCCS, NZ NCSC, and UK NCSC — published "Careful Adoption of Agentic AI Services," the first coordinated regulatory statement on autonomous agents. The headline posture is unusually blunt: until evaluation methods and standards mature, organizations should assume agentic systems will behave unexpectedly. The guidance maps the new attack surface to five risk categories — over-privileged agents, design/configuration flaws, behavioral drift, structural cascade failures, and supply-chain trust — and explicitly tells defenders not to invent a parallel security discipline: agents should be folded into existing zero-trust, defense-in-depth, and least-privilege controls. The publication landed alongside renewed warnings this week from UK NCSC about agent risks in critical infrastructure and defense.
