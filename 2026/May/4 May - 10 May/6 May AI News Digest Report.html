CAISI / NISTGoogle DeepMind, Microsoft, and xAI sign national-security pre-deployment testing agreements

5 May 2026 · Primary source: NIST / U.S. Department of Commerce

The U.S. Department of Commerce's Center for AI Standards and Innovation (CAISI), housed inside NIST, today announced agreements with Google DeepMind, Microsoft, and xAI covering pre-deployment evaluations and post-launch research on frontier AI capabilities. The new pacts extend a framework first established with OpenAI and Anthropic in 2024 to cover effectively all U.S.-headquartered frontier labs. Per CAISI and the participating companies, the labs will hand over models with reduced or removed safeguards so government red-teamers can probe cyber-offense, CBRN, and infrastructure-attack risk before public release. CAISI says it has now run 40+ such evaluations. Microsoft framed the move as a parallel to its existing AI Security Institute relationship in the U.K.; CNBC and CNN read it as the Trump administration's clearest move yet to claim a permanent seat in the frontier-launch loop. The announcement lands five days after the Pentagon published a separate $200 M classified-AI vendor slate that pointedly excluded Anthropic.

Standard IntelligenceFDM-1: a video-trained, "fully general" computer-action model emerges with $75 M from Sequoia and Spark

5 May 2026 · Primary source: Standard Intelligence (si.inc)

Six-person Seattle/SF lab Standard Intelligence announced FDM-1 — a foundation model trained directly on raw video of humans operating computers, with no LLM scaffolding and no annotated screenshots in the loop — along with a $75 M Series A led by Sequoia and Spark Capital, with angel checks from Andrej Karpathy. The model was trained on 11 M hours of screen recordings, larger than any publicly disclosed computer-use dataset, and Standard says its video encoder compresses ~2 hours of 30 FPS video into 1 M tokens. Founders Galen Mead and Devansh Pandey — both Atlas Fellows who left undergrad — pitch FDM-1 as a structural alternative to today's tool-use scaffolds (the same week a popular arXiv preprint, "Unveiling the Tool-Use Tax in LLM Agents," empirically quantified the latency and accuracy cost of those scaffolds). The bet: pixel-level pretraining transfers to any GUI a human can use.

Developer Tools & Security

OllamaChained Windows auto-updater flaws turn local LLM tooling into a persistent RCE vector

5 May 2026 · Primary source: Striga / CERT Polska / Help Net Security

Researchers at security firm Striga disclosed two unpatched vulnerabilities in Ollama for Windows — CVE-2026-42248 and CVE-2026-42249 — that, when chained, allow an unauthenticated attacker on the same network to plant a persistent executable that runs at every login by hijacking the Ollama auto-updater. CERT Polska published a national advisory on 29 April; vulnerable versions span Ollama for Windows 0.12.10 through 0.17.5, the build family covering most local-LLM developer setups shipped over the last six months. As of today no patched build exists; recommended mitigations are to disable the auto-updater, restrict outbound update traffic, or fall back to the WSL/Linux build. The flaw class is increasingly common as local-inference tooling proliferates: auto-update channels written for hobby projects are now sitting on every AI developer's laptop.
