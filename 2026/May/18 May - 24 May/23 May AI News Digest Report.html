22 May 2026 · Primary source: SEC filing (confidential) reported by CNBC, Axios, Fortune

OpenAI confidentially submitted its S-1 registration statement to the SEC on Friday, 22 May, beginning the formal path toward a public listing as early as September 2026 and no later than Q4. The deal is being led by Goldman Sachs and Morgan Stanley, with a valuation band reported between $852 B and $1 T — what would be the largest technology IPO on record. Underneath the headline number: OpenAI is running at roughly $25 B annualized revenue as of March (~$2 B/month), with 50 M consumer subscribers and 9 M business users, but reportedly lost $1.22 for every $1 of revenue in Q1 2026. Because the filing is confidential, the financials remain sealed until ~15 days before the roadshow, but the move locks in OpenAI as the benchmark by which every other frontier-AI revenue model will be priced this fall. The filing also lands the same week SpaceX disclosed an $1.25 B/month compute deal with rival Anthropic in its own S-1 — a reminder that the two leading labs are now public-markets infrastructure customers, not just competitors.

White HouseTrump pulls AI executive-order signing at the last minute

21–22 May 2026 · Primary source: White House remarks; Washington Post / CBS / CNBC

President Trump postponed a planned signing ceremony on Thursday afternoon for an executive order that would have created a voluntary framework for frontier-AI labs to share advanced models with the federal government before public release, giving agencies up to 90 days for pre-deployment security review. Invitations had already gone out to OpenAI, Anthropic, Google, and other CEOs. Trump said publicly he “didn't like certain aspects” and worried the order could become “a blocker” on U.S. leadership over China; reporting from CNBC and the Washington Post points to AI & crypto advisor David Sacks as the most vocal internal opponent. With the EO shelved, the only durable U.S. federal lever on pre-release model evaluation is back to being the voluntary US-AISI agreements signed under the Biden administration. The administration's Office of the National Cyber Director hinted that separate, narrower AI-security initiatives are still in flight.

MicrosoftTwo Defender zero-days under active exploitation; CISA adds to KEV

21–22 May 2026 · Primary source: Microsoft Security Response Center / CISA KEV

Microsoft confirmed two actively exploited vulnerabilities in the Microsoft Malware Protection Engine that ships with Microsoft Defender: CVE-2026-41091 is a local privilege-elevation flaw caused by Defender improperly resolving symbolic links before accessing files (a classic TOCTOU pattern in a privileged engine), and CVE-2026-45498 can be triggered to put the engine into a denial-of-service state, effectively disarming the endpoint protection of any machine where it runs. CISA added both CVEs to the Known Exploited Vulnerabilities catalog on 22 May, which means federal civilian agencies have a hard remediation deadline. Most environments will receive the fix automatically through MMPE definition updates, but air-gapped Windows 10/11 fleets, locked Win11-IoT images, and Defender-for-Endpoint installs with disabled cloud-delivered protection need to be checked by hand.

SplunkAI Toolkit search-filter bypass plus Enterprise/Cloud DoS chain land patches

20–22 May 2026 · Primary source: Splunk Security Advisories

Splunk's May security train shipped three notable CVEs that landlords of Splunk Enterprise and Splunk Cloud Platform should pay attention to today. CVE-2026-20238 (CVSS 6.5) is the most interesting from an AI-platform perspective: Splunk's AI Toolkit mutates the default user role's search filter, and because Splunk combines inherited search filters with OR, the AI Toolkit's broader filter overrides tighter, more restrictive role filters — meaning low-privilege users can effectively read indices they were never meant to see. CVE-2026-20239 (CVSS 7.5) leaks session cookies and HTTP response bodies into the _internal index via unsanitized TCP-channel buffer logging, and CVE-2026-20240 (CVSS 7.1) lets a non-admin user trigger arbitrary directory renames through the coldToFrozen.sh script. Patched releases are Splunk Enterprise 10.2.2, 10.0.5, 9.4.11, 9.3.12 plus the matched Splunk Cloud maintenance trains.
